Chewsy: Case Study

Chewsy is the judgment-free solution for the food enthusiast. We’ve trimmed the fat by removing paid reviews and irrelevant comments. We've boiled your search down to one simple principle: What looks good to you? With just a swipe, you’re one picture away from your next memorable meal.

The Problem:

Many people are relying on the opinions of others to fuel their restaurant choices. The trouble with this method is that it does not take the data pool’s taste into account. As a result, we are using objective mile markers to provide subjective recommendations. My goal was to build a new way to search for your next meal by taking away the din of reviews and comments and instead, honing in on what really matters… your own personal taste.

Example 1:

When asked, most people will admit that they search for food by review. This may be fine for many restaurants but 5 stars for one person might be 1 star to another. Here we have an example of the well known fast-food franchise Subway and the local Nashville favorite and independently owned Clawson’s Pub and Deli. Clawson lovers would most-likely argue that Subway should be 1 star since it isn’t fresh, local, or independently owned. Subway lovers might say Subway is 5 stars because it’s consistent, full of options, and not as fancy or busy as Clawson’s. Both are valid, but neither helps the reader since they don’t know what the diner’s taste is.

Example 2:

After filtering by rating, many diners will also turn to the comments to get a better and more human sense of the restaurant. Sometimes the reviews are relevant to the food, while other times the reviews have to do with personal interactions that are irrelevant to the reader. In the first example, the diner complains about portion size at an upscale restaurant. To some diners that may seem like a valid argument, but to others, they may think she’s missing the point. The second example showcases a review that has nothing to do with the food or even the service. It appears there was poor communication on the diner side but all the blame is put on the restaurant. It also appears the diner didn’t end up eating at the restaurant so their review appears irrelevant.

Example 3:

Finally, we have an example of how generic search doesn’t fully describe the quality or genre of food. Both of these restaurants are presented when the diner searches for “burgers” in Nashville, TN. Both are great options but without pictures, the diner wouldn’t have any context of what kind of burger they serve. Some may prefer a greasy-spoon diner style burger while another may prefer a more upscale version. If searching by name, review, or comments the diner wouldn’t be able to properly decide. Searching by image only allows the diner to see exactly what kind of burger they’re getting.

My Role:

I am the creator, designer, and developer behind Chewsy. I built Chewsy as a single-page web application using React.js, implemented authorization with Auth0, styled it with Reactstrap and CSS3, and deployed it with Heroku. All information is provided thanks to Yelp’s Fusion API.

Wireframing with Adobe XD CC

My goal was to create an intuitive, streamlined interface in which any diner could navigate with confidence. I also wanted the app to look and feel great in both mobile and desktop formats. Buttons should be large enough to tap or click, pictures should be scalable, and forms should be easy to read and fill. During development I also came to the conclusion that the original name "Tender" was too "on the nose" and could cause the diner to not take it seriously due to the pun. Tender also has a meat connotation which might turn off some diners. Chewsy was chosen because of its playful nature, connection to the purpose of the product, and fun mouth feel.
